Rule of Economies

The Rule of Economies is the concept that economies are used to keep Actors from subverting Accords. There are rules that need to be followed for an Actor to collect their allotment of Breath from the nearby Dragon-Temple, and an Actor's actions should only be restricted if they threaten the Accords.

Think of it from a writing perspective; why should the Table not be allowed to collect as much wealth as possible? Well, with that wealth they could easily hire others to do the hard work for them. Their Actors could sit back and enjoy the benefits of the work other Actors are putting in, with the Narrative reduced to raw mechanical reports from their subordinates.

That's not to say you can't make this style of play interesting; a group of nobles managing a kingdom and its workers could be just as thrilling as playing a team of adventurers. But if that style of play subverts attention from the Accords, and the Accords form the backbone of the Narrative, then the Directors have a problem which requires solving.

The Rule of Economies works to make this narratively interesting; goods are divided into negligible expenses, affordable expenses, lifestyle expenditures, apocalyptic expenditures and economic ventures.

Negligible expenses would slow down the Narrative if they were to be tallied up; unless an Actor is so poor they could barely afford a matchbox, we can assume that some meals and shelter are covered by their earnings or savings. It would slow down the pace of the game to assume otherwise; unless the Narrative specifically concerns an Actor's wealth that day, or a Player wishes to engage in impoverishment play, ignore these expenses. Cults usually provide all of these benefits as Assets and produce them with Geomancy, so there's no concern about these goods running out quickly.

Affordable expenses are expenses which are luxurious but not outside of an Actor's means. Buying a movie ticket, paying for a phone bill or building a comfortable pair of boots are usually not against the Accords of the local Dragon-Temple. Any Mortal who saved even part of their paycheck should be able to make these payments; they are negligible. Affordable does not mean accessible; these goods will require some effort to procure, though not enough effort to invoke risk.

Lifestyle expenditures are general markers for 'quality of life'; food, shelter, entertainment. Lifestyle expenditures are an Actor's way of improving their lot in life; to improve their lifestyle, they will need to get a promotion at work, patch up the drafty hole in their wall, or become a better hunter. Paying for a lifestyle expenditure should be a reward that Directors hand out for subverting an Apocalypse; adding a new ice cream machine to theirSanctuary to thank the Table for taking on Fascists is the polite and expected behavior. Players should notify their Directors that they want to spend an Act working on improving their Actor's lifestyle before making any expenditures of this value. Directors should be willing to give one or two lifestyle improvements an Act, especially if any Dances concern Dragon's Breath.

Apocalyptic expenditures are short-term goals; they're effectively heists, a fight over a resource or benefit that inherently subverts the Accords of the local Dragon-Temples. These are rare and powerful artifacts, or the deeds to million-dollar buildings; at least one Apocalypse will be necessary to secure these goods for the Table. All apocalyptic expenditures are opposed; even if no Cult took interest in the goods before the Table did, a Director should pick an existing Cult to make a rival play for the goods the Table seeks. Likewise, if a Cult seeks an artifact that Act, the Table should be given an opportunity to subvert their apocalyptic expenditure.

Economic ventures are long-term goals for the Table that cannot simply be broken down into single purchases. Funding a rebellion isn't cheap, and it's not the kind of thing that can be done over a single Act; it will require painstaking coordination and several apocalyptic expenditures to execute well. These are the goals that the Table sets to overwhelm Fascists; they are not simple expenses an Actor should be able to pay for with money. Economic ventures inherently subvert the Accords of a Dragon-Temple, and so some negotiation with Priestesses should be expected as part of the process of making the purchase and these ventures should be carefully scrutinized by the Directors. Don't just plot out financial incentives on a graph; try to make it fun for the Table, and try to break down the major barriers into four objectives: acquiring Scales to protect the investment, getting the approval of the local Priestesses, and organizing the Cult to guard the new venture. Lastly, start an Apocalypse to finalize the deal!
