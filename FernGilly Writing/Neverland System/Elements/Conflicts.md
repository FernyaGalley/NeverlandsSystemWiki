A **Conflict** occurs when an [[Actors|Actor]] has a need which cannot be met without inconveniencing another [[Game Mechanics|Element]] of the [[Neverlands System]]. If the Actor can simply retrieve a [[Props|Prop]] from a counter-top, this is not a [[Conflicts|Conflict]]; no other Element of the System is inconvenienced by their behavior. Inconveniences must require that one or both parties of a Conflict step aside so that the other can act according to their own wishes.

Conflict is only initiated when the inconvenience is irreconcilable; if either party to a Conflict backs off before the Conflict begins, the Conflict does not begin. As for minor disagreements and squabbles between bickering housemates or beleaguered bureaucrats, Conflict may occur so fast and unwittingly that no party even realizes Conflict has occurred. In text, we refer to this as **informal conflict,** since both parties have resolved their Conflict before Conflict could be formalized. But when is a Conflict formalized?

**Formal Conflict** begins when the [[Directors]] believe it would be more interesting to engage in a protracted discussion or battle with the other [[Players]] at the table. Formal Conflict needs stakes, and it requires involved parties to care deeply about the topic at hand. Formal Conflict opens up the [[Racks|Rack]] for play, and the Directors begin Formal Conflict by following the steps in the [[Order of Conflict Resolution]].

When Formal Conflict is in play, there are **Open Conflict States** (otherwise known as Open States) and **Closed Conflict States** (otherwise known as Closed States). Players and Directors should consult the [[Conflict State Handbook]] to determine whether Conflict is in a Closed or Open State, but know that **the Directors decide if Conflict is in an Open State or not.**

During an Open State, any Player at the Table who has [[to enjoin|enjoined]] themselves to the Conflict may make Statements and play them to the Rack. Playing a Statement to the Rack is risky since it opens your Actor up to attacks, but rewarding if the Statement makes it to [[Canons|Canon]]. Among other benefits, once you've made a Statement and it has been [[to canonize|canonized]], Players can start playing [[Cues]] and [[Strikes]] to pin the other Actors in the Conflict.

During a Closed State, Players should listen closely to the Directors as they narrate what happens. Directors may ask Players to speak during a Closed State, but no Statements may be played to the Rack until Conflict is in an Open State again. Examples of a Closed State include whenever another Player is making a Statement, when a Statement on the top of the Rack is being read into Canon, and when another Player enjoins the Conflict with one of their Actors.

Conflict concludes when **any party enjoined to the Conflict is unwilling to continue Conflict.** At the end of Conflict, each pinned Actor is inflicted with a Condition based on their Position when they were pinned. Actors may have their opinions swayed, be inflicted with injuries, or even mutate and change in magical and unpredictable ways, but all Players involved should consent to the results. If Conflict ended because all Actors sharing a critical Position were pinned, then all of the Actors who shared that critical Position are inflicted with yet another Condition. This Condition must be the same for all Actors, and Directors should select an appropriate Condition.

There's nothing stopping a Player or Director from immediately issuing another request for Conflict, especially if they feel like they have the upper hand. Directors are not required to empty the Rack or Canon if there are subsequent Conflicts, but it is a given that the Table may remove old and unrelated Statements from Canon; the previous Conflict is over, after all. The party initiating the subsequent Conflict must claim a new Position, and then if any Actor voices their disagreement with this new Position, those Actor(s) are immediately drawn into a new Conflict.

While these rules may look burdensome and confusing on their surface, after a single round of play it should be apparent that Conflict in the Neverlands System should look like something between witty sword banter and a courtroom brawl. The Directors will act as judges, overseeing each move taken by the Players as they attempt to win over against the arguments of fascists, witches and fae terrorists.

Those arguments may be sophistry, or they may be fists of flame and fury; whatever the case may be, we recommend Players sharpen their ability to debate by reading their Cues and figuring out new ways to use their abilities. Learn from other Players at the Table, figure out how to disrupt troublesome Statements and look for tricky ways to word your Statements so you don't get caught flat-footed. Think on your feet and you'll be sharp as a tack in no time.
