*Laconic: The Table should never lie to other Players about the game state.*

There are few faster ways to cause [[Discomfort]] than to lie to someone else. It's more than just telling [[Fictions|Fiction]]; intentionally misrepresenting the [[Statuses|State]] of [[Canons|Canon]] is cruel, if not unethical. Imagine that a [[Players|Player]] is doing everything right; they use the optimal [[Cues]] at the perfect time, they've done extensive research and pieced together a ten-minute long argument to defeat [[Fascists|Fascism]] once and for all.

Just as the [[Tables|Table]] [[to cast|casts]] their final [[Cues|Cue]], the [[Directors|Director]] taps the shoulder of the Player, revealing that their arguments were actually all based on lies, so each one of the Cues they played that relied on [[Statements]] being [[Facts]] now fails in [[Arbitration]]. The Table loses the [[Conflicts|Conflict]], the Fascists throw them all in prison, and everyone feels like this whole Conflict was a *miserable waste of their time.* It would not be shocking to see that Player quit soon after!

This is the consequence of dishonest play!

As a [[Game Mechanics|Rule]], whenever a Player [[to speak|speaks]] in [[Fictions|Fiction]], they need to announce that they are [[to lie|lying]] or playing a Cue which enables them [[to speak]] in [[Fictions|Fiction]]. Players should *always* [[to denote|denote]] Fiction, and Directors who want to tell a mystery should use [[Alternatives]] to present duplicitous Statements instead of labeling them as [[Facts]]. Directors should get in the habit of denoting any Statement with a remote possibility of being a lie as [[Fictions|Fiction]], [[Alternatives]] or even [[Opinions]].

Some readers are probably balking at the idea of [[to reveal|revealing]] a mystery to Players. 'What do you mean, the existence of a locked room is mere Opinion? Isn't that as good as admitting there is no locked room in this whodunnit?'

To these readers, I speak plainly; there is no such thing as a [[Locked Room Theory]]. Beyond that, Players are writers coallaborating towards a grander [[Narratives|Narrative]]; there must be no deception between collaborators. If your mystery cannot be told without [[to gaslight|gaslighting]] your friends, your mystery is not worth reading. As a mystery writer, the first rule of mysteries is [[to foreshadow]] every clue; to clearly spell out each of the suspects, so nobody is surprised; to use [[Rule of Drama|dramatic irony]] to keep the audience in suspense.

If a [[Scenes|Scene]] seems too obvious, subvert expectations; supply just enough information that any of the alternative hypotheses could be true! An excellent Director could even have *both* possibilities exist as equally valid Alternatives, and let the Players decide which version of reality makes the most sense; you are not the only writer at the Table, and relying on Players can at times make for incredibly satisfying conclusions.
